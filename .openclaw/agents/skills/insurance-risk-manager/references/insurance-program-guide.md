# Insurance Program Guide

## Purpose

Comprehensive reference for managing a CRE portfolio insurance program covering all coverage lines, renewal marketing, and claims management. Written for institutional owner-operators managing $100M-$5B in assets.

## Property Insurance Program Components

### 1. Property/Casualty Coverage

#### Replacement Cost Determination

Replacement cost is the cornerstone of property insurance. Methods for determining replacement cost:

1. **Marshall Valuation Service (MVS)**: Industry-standard cost estimator. Input building class, quality, size, height, and location. Output: replacement cost per SF.
2. **Appraisal**: Commissioned replacement cost estimate by certified appraiser. Most accurate but expensive ($2-10K per property).
3. **Insurance carrier valuation**: Carrier's own estimation tool during underwriting. Free but may be conservative.
4. **Construction cost indexing**: Apply ENR or RS Means construction cost index to original construction cost. Quick but imprecise for older buildings.

**Best practice**: Use MVS for annual portfolio updates. Commission appraisals every 3-5 years for large assets. Index between appraisals.

**Recent trends**: Construction costs have inflated 25-40% since 2020 in many markets. If insured values have not been updated, coinsurance penalties are a real risk.

#### Coinsurance Penalty Avoidance

Most commercial property policies include a coinsurance clause (typically 80%, 90%, or 100%).

**How coinsurance works**:
- Policy requires insured to maintain coverage equal to X% of replacement cost
- If insured value < X% of replacement cost at time of loss, penalty applies
- Penalty formula: (Insured Value / Required Value) x Loss - Deductible = Payment

**Example**:
- Building replacement cost: $50M
- Coinsurance: 90%
- Required insured value: $45M (90% x $50M)
- Actual insured value: $35M (owner hasn't updated)
- Loss: $10M
- Payment: ($35M / $45M) x $10M - deductible = $7.78M - deductible
- **Penalty: $2.22M+ underpaid**

**Avoidance strategies**:
1. Agreed Amount endorsement: carrier agrees to waive coinsurance in exchange for a statement of values. Requires annual update.
2. Blanket coverage: insure entire portfolio under a single blanket limit equal to total replacement cost. No per-building coinsurance.
3. Annual replacement cost updates: update values annually using MVS or indexing.

### 2. General Liability

| Component | Standard Limit | Institutional Target |
|-----------|---------------|---------------------|
| Each Occurrence | $1,000,000 | $1,000,000 |
| General Aggregate | $2,000,000 | $2,000,000 |
| Products/Completed Operations | $2,000,000 | $2,000,000 |
| Personal & Advertising Injury | $1,000,000 | $1,000,000 |
| Damage to Rented Premises | $300,000 | $500,000 |
| Medical Expense | $5,000 | $10,000 |

**Key endorsements**:
- Additional insured for lenders, JV partners, and PM companies
- Waiver of subrogation as required by leases and management agreements
- Per-location aggregate (prevents a claim at one property from exhausting portfolio aggregate)

**Premium benchmarks** (per unit or per 1,000 SF):
- Multifamily: $40-$80/unit
- Office: $0.15-$0.30/SF
- Retail: $0.20-$0.40/SF
- Industrial: $0.08-$0.15/SF

### 3. Umbrella/Excess Liability

Provides additional limits above GL, auto, and employer's liability.

| Portfolio Size | Recommended Umbrella/Excess |
|---------------|---------------------------|
| < $100M TIV | $5-10M |
| $100-500M TIV | $10-15M |
| $500M-$1B TIV | $15-25M |
| > $1B TIV | $25-50M+ |

**Structure**: Umbrella (broader coverage, follows form with extensions) vs. Excess (follows form only, narrower). First layer is umbrella, additional layers are excess. Lead umbrella typically $5-10M, then stacked excess in $5-10M increments.

**Pricing**: Lead umbrella is most expensive layer. Subsequent excess layers cheaper per dollar of limit (inverse relationship to expected loss frequency at higher attachment points).

### 4. Builder's Risk (During Construction)

**Coverage scope**:
- All-risk, replacement cost basis
- Materials in transit (from supplier to site)
- Materials stored off-site (at fabricator, warehouse)
- Soft costs: A&E fees, permit fees, financing costs (interest carry), lost rental income
- Testing and commissioning
- Delay in completion (extends lost income if construction is delayed by covered peril)

**Standard exclusions** (negotiate back where possible):
- Named windstorm in coastal areas (sublimit, often $5-10M)
- Flood (sublimit, often $5-10M)
- Earthquake (sublimit in seismic zones)
- Design error (insurable via professional liability)
- Faulty workmanship (insurable via contractor's errors)
- Existing structures (if renovation, need separate coverage for existing building)

**Duration**: Notice to proceed through substantial completion + 60-90 days for punchlist/closeout.

**Named insureds**: Owner, GC, all subcontractors of every tier, architect, lender.

**Transition to permanent**: Builder's risk terminates at TCO/CO. Permanent property coverage must bind before builder's risk expires. Overlap by 30 days minimum. Coordinate with property insurance broker to pre-bind permanent coverage conditioned on TCO.

### 5. OCIP vs CCIP Comparison

| Feature | OCIP | CCIP |
|---------|------|------|
| Full Name | Owner-Controlled Insurance Program | Contractor-Controlled Insurance Program |
| Who procures | Owner | General Contractor |
| Insurance cost in bids | Stripped out (additive) | Included in GC contract |
| Typical savings | 1-3% of hard cost | Savings retained by GC |
| Administrative burden | High (owner manages enrollment) | Low (GC manages) |
| Minimum project size | $50M+ (economies of scale) | $20M+ |
| Claims control | Owner (advantage in litigation) | GC |
| Wrap-up coverage | GL, WC, excess, builder's risk | Same |
| Best for | Sophisticated owner, multiple primes | Single GC, simpler project |

**OCIP Cost-Benefit Analysis Template**:
1. Estimate total insurance cost if each contractor carries own policies
2. Estimate OCIP program premium (GL, WC, excess, admin)
3. Calculate net savings: #1 - #2 - admin costs
4. Threshold: savings > $200K and project > $50M = OCIP likely worthwhile

### 6. Terrorism Insurance (TRIA)

The Terrorism Risk Insurance Act provides a federal backstop for certified acts of terrorism.

- **Mandatory offer**: Carriers must offer terrorism coverage. Insured can accept or reject.
- **Premium**: Typically 2-5% of base property premium for most assets. Higher for iconic/high-profile assets in gateway cities (NYC, DC, LA, Chicago, SF).
- **Federal backstop**: After carrier deductible (20% of direct earned premium), federal government pays 80% of losses above deductible up to $100B industry aggregate.
- **Recommendation**: Always maintain TRIA coverage for institutional portfolios. The premium is modest relative to catastrophic loss potential. Rejecting TRIA may violate lender requirements.

**NBCR coverage** (Nuclear, Biological, Chemical, Radiological): Not covered by standard TRIA. Requires separate policy if desired (expensive, limited market).

### 7. Flood Insurance

| Zone | Description | Insurance Requirement |
|------|-------------|---------------------|
| A, AE, AH, AO | High-risk flood zone | Mandatory for federally-backed loans |
| V, VE | Coastal high-risk (wave action) | Mandatory, higher premiums |
| B, X (shaded) | Moderate risk | Not required, recommended |
| C, X (unshaded) | Low risk | Not required |

**NFIP vs Private**:
- NFIP maximum: $500K building, $500K contents (commercial)
- Private flood: higher limits available, often broader coverage
- For institutional assets: NFIP is often insufficient. Procure private excess flood above NFIP.
- Check: some lenders require NFIP as primary even with private available

### 8. Earthquake Coverage

- **Exposure**: Highest in California (Zone 4), Pacific Northwest, New Madrid (Midwest), Charleston SC
- **Pricing**: Deductibles are percentage-based (typically 5-15% of insured value, not a dollar amount)
- **Probable Maximum Loss (PML)**: Seismic engineering study estimates expected loss from a specified earthquake scenario. Portfolio PML determines insurance limit needed.
- **Decision framework**: If PML x probability > annual premium, insure. For California assets, earthquake coverage is standard. For low-seismic zones, self-insure with catastrophic excess.

## Coverage Adequacy Testing Methodology

### Annual Adequacy Review Process

**Step 1: Replacement Cost Update**
- Run MVS valuation on every asset
- Compare to current schedule of values
- Flag any property >10% underinsured
- Update statement of values for agreed amount endorsement

**Step 2: Revenue/NOI Exposure**
- Calculate business interruption exposure: annual NOI x extended period of indemnity (typically 12-18 months)
- Verify BI limits cover at least 18 months of NOI plus extra expense
- For development: verify delay in completion coverage matches projected lease-up timeline

**Step 3: Liability Benchmarking**
- Compare per-unit and per-SF liability limits to REIT peer group
- Assess umbrella adequacy against worst-case litigation scenario
- Review claim frequency and severity trends

**Step 4: Catastrophic Scenario Testing**
- Model total loss at most valuable asset
- Model portfolio-wide catastrophe (hurricane, earthquake)
- Verify aggregate limits cover combined exposure
- Confirm reinsurance backing of carrier (for large portfolios)

**Step 5: Gap Documentation**
- Produce coverage gap matrix: property x coverage type
- Quantify financial exposure per gap
- Prioritize: mandatory gaps (lender/code required) first, then material financial exposure, then best-practice

### Broker Marketing Requirements

Require broker to:
1. Submit to minimum 5 carriers per major coverage line
2. Provide side-by-side quote comparison with standardized terms
3. Disclose all broker compensation (commission, contingent commission, fees)
4. Provide carrier financial strength analysis (A.M. Best, S&P)
5. Recommend coverage enhancements with cost-benefit
6. Present loss-sensitive alternatives (large deductible, self-insured retention, captive)

### Loss-Sensitive Program Considerations

For portfolios > $500M TIV, evaluate:

| Structure | Premium Reduction | Cash Flow Impact | Risk |
|-----------|------------------|-----------------|------|
| Large deductible ($100-500K) | 10-25% | Cash retained until loss | Volatility in loss years |
| Self-insured retention ($250K-$1M) | 15-35% | Cash retained | Need claims admin |
| Captive insurance | 20-40% | Investment income on reserves | Setup cost $100-300K, ongoing admin |
| Group captive | 15-30% | Shared with members | Less control |

**Decision rule**: If 5-year average annual losses < 40% of premium, a loss-sensitive structure likely saves money. Requires adequate balance sheet to absorb retained losses.

## Claims Management Best Practices

### First Notice of Loss Protocol

1. **Immediate** (within 24 hours): Notify broker and carrier of any claim likely to exceed $10K or involve injury
2. **Documentation**: Photographs, incident reports, witness statements, police reports
3. **Mitigation**: Take reasonable steps to prevent further damage (board up, emergency repairs)
4. **Reservation of rights**: Do not admit liability. Do not authorize repairs beyond emergency mitigation without carrier approval
5. **Tracking**: Log claim in central claims database with: date of loss, date reported, property, coverage line, estimated reserve, adjuster assigned

### Claims Advocacy

- Broker should advocate on owner's behalf with adjuster
- For losses > $100K: consider hiring public adjuster (fee: 5-10% of settlement)
- For losses > $500K: consider retaining own counsel to negotiate with carrier
- Track: time from first notice to payment, settlement amount vs. reserve, carrier responsiveness

### Subrogation

- Owner's carrier may pursue recovery from responsible third party (contractor, tenant, vendor)
- Waiver of subrogation in leases and contracts limits carrier's ability to subrogate
- Trade-off: waiver of subrogation prevents carrier from suing your tenant/contractor, but may increase premium slightly
- Standard practice: include waiver of subrogation in all leases and construction contracts
